Why altitude matters

Old satellites were too far away

Traditional satellites orbit 35,786 km up — so far that signals take ~600 ms to make the round trip. Starlink orbits at 550 km, cutting that to just 20–40 ms. That's the difference between choppy video calls and smooth ones.

Step by step

What happens when you load a webpage

Your data makes a round trip through space in under 100 milliseconds. Here's exactly how.

01
You click a link
Your device wraps your request into small data packets and sends them to the Starlink dish on your roof via your home network.
~0 ms
02
Dish fires a signal to space
The dish points itself at the nearest satellite and beams your data up using a Ku-band radio signal. At 550 km away, the signal takes only ~1.8 ms to arrive.
~2 ms uplink
03
Satellites pass the data along
If the website's server is far away, satellites relay your data to each other using laser beams — crossing oceans through space instead of undersea cables.
~5–15 ms transit
04
Data comes back down to Earth
A ground station receives the signal and sends your request into the regular internet, which routes it to the server hosting the website you want.
~2 ms downlink
05
The page loads on your screen
The server's response travels back the same way in reverse. Total time for the whole round trip: 20–40 ms — fast enough for smooth video calls and online gaming.
20–40 ms total

Got questions?

Common questions, plain answers

It uses a "phased array" — thousands of tiny antennas that work together to electronically steer a beam of radio waves, with no motors or moving parts. The software figures out where the satellite is and adjusts the beam in milliseconds. It's similar technology to modern fighter jet radars.
Because Starlink flies much lower, each satellite can only "see" a small patch of Earth at a time — roughly a 1,000 km circle. An old geostationary satellite can see nearly a third of the planet from 35,786 km up. To keep one satellite visible above you at all times, you need a much larger constellation of low-flying ones.
Yes, for certain long-distance routes. Light travels about 40% faster through the vacuum of space than through glass fiber. For a route like Tokyo to London, beaming the signal through space via laser-linked satellites can actually be faster than running it through undersea cables. Finance companies already pay for this speed advantage.
No — at 550 km altitude, there's just enough atmosphere to gradually drag them back down. A dead satellite will naturally burn up in the atmosphere within a few years. SpaceX also gives each satellite thrusters to steer around debris and de-orbit in a controlled way if needed. The main concern is actually light pollution for astronomers.
Heavy rain can weaken the signal slightly — this is called "rain fade." It's usually minor, but a severe storm can cause brief drops in speed. Snow and ice on the dish is a bigger issue, which is why Starlink dishes have a built-in heater to melt accumulation and keep the signal clear.
